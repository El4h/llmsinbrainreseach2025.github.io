Using Large Language Models for Brain Research:
From Theory to Application

Large language models (LLMs) are emerging as transformative tools in neuroscience and psychological research, offering novel approaches to understanding brain function, and accelerating discovery cycles. These models have demonstrated their use in predicting experimental outcomes, generating experimental stimuli, and identifying research gaps.

Despite their potential, challenges remain in model interpretability and application to truly innovative research paradigms. Early career researchers stand to benefit significantly from understanding these tools, as they can leverage LLMs to enhance their own research methodologies and explore new frontiers in neuroscience and psychology. This workshop aims to provide a comprehensive introduction to LLMs tailored to researchers of all experience levels. Through this workshop, participants can gain foundational knowledge of large language models and their applications in neuroscience and psychological research, develop practical skills for leveraging LLMs in experimental design, data analysis, and hypothesis generation, network with peers and experts to foster collaborations that advance interdisciplinary research.

Join us for a 1.5-day workshop on "Large Language Models (LLMs) in Brain Research", bringing together experts at the forefront of cognitive neuroscience and AI. The program will explore how LLMs and vision-language models relate to human brain mechanisms, how they can generate and model linguistic data, and how brain-inspired methods can improve model alignment with neural activity. Speakers will cover cutting-edge topics such as multimodal representations, brain-tuning of language models, and decoding visual scenes from neural signals. Designed for researchers of all levels, the workshop combines conceptual grounding with practical guidance on applying LLMs in cognitive and neuroscience research. Participants will gain insights, build skills, and connect across disciplines to push the boundaries of research.
